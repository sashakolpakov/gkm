\begin{abstract}
We study whether an agent can \emph{figure a game out on its own} --- discover its
perception, its mechanics, its goal, and a winning strategy --- from the rawest
possible interface, carrying only general human preconceptions, with a single
free-energy rule deciding what structure is worth keeping. The setting is ARC-AGI-3
keyboard games played offline through their runtime. Our agent is given nothing but
\texttt{step(action)}$\to$\emph{frame}, a scalar reward (\emph{levels completed}), and
a clone for lookahead. A proposer, prompted with human world-priors, \emph{writes its
own program} to play; a candidate is admitted only if it verifiably improves on the
incumbent under a free energy $\F = R + \lambda C$ that trades reward against
description length. We connect this to Kolmogorov complexity and Solomonoff induction
(the $C$ term), to Schmidhuber's G\"odel machine and PowerPlay (self-rewriting under an
empirical proof of improvement, driven by compression-progress novelty), and to a
colimit-cone view of skills (``legs'') composed into solutions. Empirically, the
promoted GKM artifacts solve \texttt{wa30} through level~9 and \texttt{ls20} through
level~7, replay-validated from their recorded action paths. The \texttt{wa30}
artifact reaches level~9 in 596 moves with total marginal complexity 1243; the
\texttt{ls20} artifact reaches level~7 in 393 moves with total marginal complexity
362. The preserved WIP snapshots show how these solvers were obtained: early levels
were searched or written directly by the proposer, later levels were refactored into
thin players over a shared leg library, and recovered verified paths were admitted only
after independent replay. The result is not ``one monolithic script got lucky'' but a
priced growth trace: reuse is free, literal replay plans are charged, and new
mechanics appear as marginal novelty. We report the honest negatives alongside: the
result is on two local preview games, not the full ARC-AGI-3 distribution, and a
prompt-only local model is currently too weak to drive the same loop.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
ARC-AGI-3~\cite{chollet2019measure,arcagi3} poses interactive grid games whose rules are not
given: an agent sees a $64\times64$ grid of colour integers, may press one of a few
opaque buttons, and receives only a coarse reward when it completes a level. Nothing is
labelled --- not the avatar, not the objects, not the goal. Solving such a game from
scratch requires \emph{discovering} the perception, the manipulation mechanics, the
objective, and a plan, and then \emph{transferring} what was learned to later levels
and other games.
This is a deliberately hard regime: the ARC-AGI-3 report describes human test-takers
solving all environments while frontier AI systems scored below 1\% as of March
2026~\cite{arcagi3}. The immediately preceding ARC Prize 2025 report identifies
per-task refinement loops --- iterative program improvement guided by feedback --- as
the defining successful pattern on ARC-AGI-2~\cite{arcprize2025}. Our work brings that
pattern to the interactive ARC-AGI-3 setting, but with an explicit MDL/free-energy
price on new program structure. Contemporary ARC-AGI-3 baselines also point toward
explicit state tracking, exploration graphs, and executable world models rather than
pure prompt-only reasoning~\cite{arcagi3graph,arcagi3worldmodels}; GKM differs by
making the growth of those structures itself the priced object.

Our thesis is that an agent equipped with a rich stock of \emph{human preconceptions}
about the world, and disciplined by a single \emph{free-energy} selection rule, can do
this itself, with the human contribution confined to (i) the rawest game-independent
interface, (ii) the preconception priors, and (iii) the free-energy accounting. We make
three points precise. First, the interface must expose \emph{only} what is common to
all games, or it smuggles in a solution that will not transfer. Second, the reusable
structure that lets later levels be cheap must be \emph{invented by a proposer}, not
enumerated from a fixed library --- and admitted only against an empirical proof.
Third, the accounting that decides ``is this structure worth its cost'' is the same
free energy throughout, and it is exactly the object that makes transfer the parsimonious
choice.

\section{Background}
\subsection{Kolmogorov complexity, MDL, and free energy}
The (prefix) Kolmogorov complexity $K(x)$ is the length of the shortest program that
outputs $x$; Solomonoff induction weights hypotheses by $2^{-K}$, formalising Occam's
razor~\cite{kolmogorov1965,solomonoff1964,live2008}. $K$ is uncomputable, but its
description-length spirit is usable: prefer the shortest structure that explains the
data. We adopt a loss--complexity \emph{free energy}
\begin{equation}
  \F_\lambda(s) \;=\; R(s) \;+\; \lambda\,C(s),
  \label{eq:fe}
\end{equation}
where $R$ is empirical goal risk (here, unsolved levels) and $C$ is a description-length
complexity of the candidate structure $s$; $\lambda$ tunes the pressure toward compact
solutions~\cite{losscomplexity}. Equation~\eqref{eq:fe} is an MDL objective: a
structure earns its complexity only if it buys enough reduction in risk.

\subsection{Schmidhuber: self-rewriting, PowerPlay, curiosity}
Schmidhuber's G\"odel machine~\cite{schmidhuber2007godel} is an agent that may rewrite
\emph{any} part of its own code, including its learning algorithm, once it finds a proof
that the rewrite increases expected reward. PowerPlay~\cite{schmidhuber2013powerplay}
incrementally searches for the simplest task the system cannot yet solve together with a
solver for it, never forgetting old skills. Artificial curiosity rewards
\emph{compression progress}: transitions that make the agent's world model more
predictable~\cite{schmidhuber2010curiosity}. We take three ideas: (a) the agent should
\emph{write and revise its own problem-solving code}; (b) a candidate is adopted only
against a \emph{proof of improvement} --- which we discharge \emph{empirically}, by the
simulator, rather than by a theorem prover; (c) novelty/compression-progress can
\emph{steer} exploration but is priced, not free.

\subsection{The colimit-cone view of skills}
A recurring construction in this project treats few-shot competence as a diagram of
reusable behaviours (``legs'') together with priced gluings (``cones'') that compose
them into a solution, selected by free energy. In the classical form the legs are
\emph{discovered by interaction} and the cone is a search over them. Here we push the
construction to its limit: the legs are \emph{written by the proposer} as program
fragments, the cone that glues them is the proposer's own planner, and admission remains
Eq.~\eqref{eq:fe}. The essential invariant is that a reused leg is already paid for, so
composition is cheap and only genuine novelty is charged.

\section{Method}
\subsection{The rawest substrate}
A persistent failure mode is smuggling the solution into the harness as a ``primitive'':
any helper richer than the bare runtime --- a perception module, a typed object model, a
push-versus-carry mechanic, a search planner --- is the programmer solving the game, and
it does not survive a change of game \emph{type}. We therefore expose only
\begin{center}
\texttt{step(action)}$\to$\emph{frame}, \quad \emph{frame} (raw $64\times64$ grid),
\quad \emph{levels\_completed} (reward), \quad \texttt{clone()} (lookahead).
\end{center}
Nothing here names objects, colours, walls, avatars, or goals. Perceiving objects,
finding the controllable object, learning the mechanic, inducing the goal, modelling other
agents, planning, and composing manoeuvres must all be produced \emph{inside} the loop.

\subsection{Preconceptions and the proposer}
The proposer receives a system prompt of \emph{human preconceptions}: that frames contain
persistent objects; that barriers partition space into regions and reachability must be
reasoned per agent; that one object may be a controllable avatar and others autonomous
agents (helpful or adversarial) that act on every move; that cooperation can require a
hand-off at a shared boundary; that rewards are often sparse, so the agent should set
itself dense surrogate objectives and discover its actions' affordances by experiment.
These priors are deliberately \emph{non-procedural}: they say what a world tends to
contain, not how to solve any game. Given the priors and the raw substrate the proposer
\emph{writes a} \texttt{solve(env)} \emph{program} --- its perception, its mechanic
probe, its planner, its strategy. The proposer is pluggable: a small local model, or the
Claude Code agent invoked headlessly with file/shell tools and a tester, so it writes,
runs, observes failures, and iterates --- the same write/run/fix loop a programmer uses.
All reported results use the strong proposer; the small local model is presently too
weak to drive the loop (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{Admission by free energy; the leg library}
A written program is admitted only if replay lowers Eq.~\eqref{eq:fe} on the real
game. We take $R$ to be minus the number of levels reached and $C$ to be the
program's description length, with $\lambda$ small so parsimony breaks ties without
stifling a larger program that clears more levels;
compression-progress/disagreement add an intrinsic novelty term that only \emph{steers}
exploration; earlier behaviour is preserved against regression; and the simulator is the
ground-truth verifier (a candidate is replayed on a fresh environment and kept only if
the reward actually advances). Two constraints govern the loop: selection cannot be a
\emph{source} of novelty (that is the proposer's job), and discovery/strategy must be
learned inside the loop, never hand-coded.

To make later levels cheap we grow a \emph{leg library}. A shared module accumulates
small named skills (perception helpers, navigation, transport, relay-across-a-barrier);
each level's player is restricted to \emph{composing} those legs; after each level a
\emph{debrief} refactors repeated code into shared legs and logs the recurring
composition (itself a candidate higher-order leg). Crucially, the complexity term is
\emph{marginal}: $C_{\text{marg}}$ counts only \emph{new} legs plus composition glue, so a
reused leg costs nothing and Eq.~\eqref{eq:fe} literally rewards transfer. This is the
colimit cone made operational, and it must be \emph{enforced by the harness}: merely
asking the proposer to grow a library is ignored (it grows a monolith); restricting each
player to composition over a shared library, with a separate refactor pass and marginal
scoring, produces the intended behaviour.

\section{Results}
All results are replay-validated: the recorded action sequence is re-run on a fresh
environment and must reach the claimed level. The ground truth for the claims below is
the promoted GKM code/artifacts under \texttt{arc/crack\_lab/agent\_solutions/*\_legs}
plus their preserved WIP snapshots.

\paragraph{Discovery and depth (\texttt{wa30}).} The current promoted \texttt{wa30}
leg-library artifact is verified through level~9: \texttt{checkpoint.json} records
\texttt{reached=9}, \texttt{validated=true}, a 596-action final path, and total
marginal complexity 1243. Its run log reports
$\F=-9+0.02\cdot1243=15.86$. The artifact is not a single opaque replay. The promoted
\texttt{players.py} is a thin set of per-level compositions over shared legs:
early levels use named movement/use and relay skills; levels~5--8 compose ferry,
neutralise, courier-yield, and dual-socket filling legs; level~9 is a five-entry
\texttt{ferry\_each} specification. The WIP snapshots preserve the search history:
failed and interrupted proposals, reached-before-debrief states, recovered verified
path artifacts, and after-debrief refactors. In particular, the level-9 WIP debrief
shows that an initially recovered 61-action suffix was decoded into five repeated
grab--carry--release ferries and refactored into \texttt{grab\_carry\_release} and
\texttt{ferry\_each}, rather than left as an undifferentiated action string.

The current artifact also supersedes the older level~6 claim. The important qualitative
discoveries remain the same but now occur inside a longer growth trace: freezing target
regions whose cells are overwritten by delivered objects; complementing autonomous
helpers rather than competing with them; exploiting asymmetric carry collision at a
wall boundary; neutralising autonomous agents that undo progress; and recognising a
recurring ``neutralise, then deliver'' shape across later levels. The honest caveat is
also preserved: some early runs used priors and probes that were later judged too
informative about the carry family and relay concept. The promoted leg-library result is
therefore best read as a replay-validated GKM growth artifact on \texttt{wa30}, not as a
claim that every semantic hint was absent from every historical run.

\paragraph{Transfer across game type (\texttt{ls20}).} The same enforced leg-library
harness also promotes a \texttt{ls20} artifact verified through level~7:
\texttt{reached=7}, \texttt{validated=true}, a 393-action final path, and total
marginal complexity 362. This is a different game family: the early \texttt{ls20}
levels are sliding-tile track puzzles rather than pick-up-and-carry logistics. The
preserved debriefs show the agent first relying on full-frame BFS, then adding a
generic noise-mask leg when a drifting HUD broke raw-frame deduplication, then using
recovered verified paths for deeper levels where understanding combination-lock,
energy, spring, patrol, and fog-of-war mechanics was cheaper than blind search. The
same external interface and admission rule handle both games; what changes is the
library the proposer grows.

\paragraph{Leg reuse and marginal novelty.} The \texttt{ls20} promoted checkpoint gives
the cleanest marginal-complexity trace (Table~\ref{tab:marg}). Levels~1--2 are cheap
full-frame search; levels~3--4 add and reuse the noise-mask/search composition; levels
5--7 use \texttt{solve\_by\_replay} for verified paths recovered from proposer WIP. The
trace is sawtoothed rather than monotonically decreasing. Its drops
($43\to2$, then $45\to3$) are direct evidence of transfer: once a leg exists, the
next compatible level needs only glue. Its spikes ($45$, $72$, $130$) mark mechanic
transitions: a drifting HUD/noise process, then recovered plan artifacts, then the
combination-lock/display family. Level~7 drops again relative to level~6 because it
reuses part of that lock/display understanding, while adding fog-of-war mapping and a
new verified plan. This is exactly what the marginal free-energy accounting is meant
to expose: reuse is cheap when the level admits reuse, and novelty is paid for when the
game introduces new structure.

\paragraph{The complement on \texttt{wa30}: novelty tracks the game's novelty.} Running
\texttt{wa30} under the same enforced library shows the honest counterpart. The
promoted checkpoint records marginal novelty for levels~2 and~4--9 as
$30,87,405,225,145,204,147$, with total marginal complexity 1243. The omitted early
levels are already present in the promoted base state; the checkpoint records the
resume history it can audit. The qualitative pattern is still clear from the code and
WIP snapshots: \texttt{wa30} keeps introducing new logistics structure, so marginal
complexity does \emph{not} collapse. Level~4 adds a larger ferry/yield composition;
level~5 adds live-frame courier pacing; levels~6--8 share a higher-order
\texttt{neutralize\_then\_deliver} pattern over different neutralisation and delivery
subroutines; level~9 refactors a recovered path into the reusable \texttt{ferry\_each}
batch leg. Reuse-collapse is therefore a property of the \emph{game's} level structure,
not an artifact of the harness: the marginal price in Eq.~\eqref{eq:fe} is paid when
the game demands new structure and drops when a new level can be expressed as a
composition of existing legs.

\section{Discussion and current scope}\label{sec:discussion}
The architecture --- rawest substrate, preconception priors, free-energy admission,
simulator-as-verifier, marginally-priced leg library --- is general and is the
contribution. Three current bounds apply. (1) The claim is the promoted artifact result:
\texttt{wa30} through level~9 and \texttt{ls20} through level~7 on the local preview
games, not all ARC-AGI-3 games and not a private benchmark score. (2) The loop currently
needs a strong proposer: a prompt-only local model mis-reasoned two-sided reachability
under barriers even with the priors spelled out, so the interesting open question is how
weak a proposer the same harness --- priors, simulator-as-verifier, free-energy
admission --- can lift to competence. (3) Recovered verified paths are admissible under
the artifact discipline only because they are independently replay-validated and charged
as literal description length; they should not be confused with discovering a compact
mechanistic leg until a debrief refactors them, as happened for \texttt{wa30} level~9.
The same discipline governs interruption: WIP context is snapshotted on interrupt,
restore never overwrites scratch state newer than the latest snapshot, and a
proposer-mutated checkpoint is adopted only after independent replay validation ---
so the preserved growth trace stays auditable across crashes and resumes.

\paragraph{Comparison to contemporary baselines.} The ARC-AGI-3 report lists
public-environment harnesses solving \texttt{ls20}, \texttt{ft09}, and \texttt{vc33};
its paper source does not mention \texttt{wa30}~\cite{arcagi3}. The graph-exploration
baseline evaluates \texttt{ls20} but not \texttt{wa30}; at its 4{,}000-interaction
comparison point it solves 2 levels on \texttt{ls20} and reports that levels~3+ become
intractable for exhaustive exploration~\cite{arcagi3graph}. The closest comparator is
the executable-world-model baseline, which reports \texttt{ls20} 7/7 with both of its
models and \texttt{wa30} 7/9 versus 4/9 depending on the
model~\cite{arcagi3worldmodels}. GKM is therefore not unique on \texttt{ls20}; its
competitive point is \texttt{wa30} 9/9 replay-validated, together with the audit
ledger behind the promoted solver --- preserved WIP snapshots, replay validation,
marginal-complexity charges, charged literals, and leg-library refactors. This is a
qualitative comparison, not a compute-matched one; a matched head-to-head is the next
evaluation step.

Relative to the classical colimit-cone experiments, where reusable structure is enumerated
and priced, here it is \emph{invented} and priced by the same free energy; the two are
ends of one spectrum, and Table~\ref{tab:marg} is direct evidence that the artifact
records transfer and novelty in the same currency.
