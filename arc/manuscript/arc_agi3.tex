\begin{abstract}
When a coding agent solves successive tasks, the final score does not reveal whether
later solutions reuse earlier structure, introduce new abstractions, or merely retain
task-specific traces. We present an artifact-oriented protocol for auditing these
alternatives in interactive ARC-AGI-3 games. A coding proposer receives raw frames,
actions, scalar level progress, and a local cloning oracle; it writes level programs
over a shared library. Fresh replay gates promotion, while a marginal
description-length ledger records positive net growth in executable source and explicit
literals. Eight promoted artifacts are locally replay-valid and reproduce on a closed
Competition-Mode scorecard: 17.1365\% over 25 public games (37/183 levels; 1456 API
actions including resets). One unchanged game-agnostic harness produced complete paths
for \texttt{wa30} (9/9, 596 artifact actions) and \texttt{ls20} (7/7, 393), level-4
paths for five further games, and a level-1 path for \texttt{tu93}. The two complete
histories permit structural analysis. The \texttt{ls20} marginal trace
$43,2,45,3,72,130,67$ has near-zero troughs exactly where thin players invoke unchanged
search routines; larger increments retain masking code or verified plans. Preserved
intermediate states distinguish recovered action sequences from subsequent refactors.
The metric is a path-dependent source proxy, not an estimator of semantic novelty, and
the score does not measure clone-enabled discovery cost. The result is an executable,
falsifiable account of solver growth rather than a compute-matched performance claim.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} ARC-AGI-3; Kolmogorov structure function; categorical
colimit; minimum description length; program synthesis; artifact evaluation.

\section{Introduction}
ARC-AGI-3~\cite{chollet2019measure,arcagi3} consists of interactive grid games whose
rules are withheld. An agent observes a $64\times64$ array of colour values, chooses
among a small set of opaque actions, and receives sparse feedback when a level is
completed. It must infer useful perceptual categories, mechanics, objectives, and plans
from interaction. Successive levels create a second problem beyond task completion:
determining whether competence transfers or whether each level is solved anew.

This distinction is obscured by aggregate scores. A solver that composes a previously
learned routine and one that stores a newly recovered action sequence may reach the same
level, but they provide different evidence about generalization. The distinction is
especially relevant for coding agents, whose iterative write--execute--revise loops are
effective on ARC-style tasks~\cite{arcprize2025}, and for ARC-AGI-3 approaches based on
exploration graphs or executable world models~\cite{arcagi3graph,arcagi3worldmodels}.
Their executable artifacts make structural growth observable, but only if changes are
recorded and charged rather than hidden in a cumulative program.

We study the following question: \emph{Can marginal description-length accounting make
reuse, new solver structure, and literal memorization distinguishable in the growth
history of an interactive agent?} We investigate it through a verifier-driven
program-growth harness in which a coding proposer writes level solvers over a shared
library. The game runtime validates behavioral progress, while a source-based ledger
records retained growth at each level. A diagnostic free-energy quantity combines
replay progress with cumulative historical charge; in the present implementation it is
an audit statistic rather than an optimizer over a controlled candidate set. A separate
retained-description functional organizes the structure-function analysis below.

The paper makes three contributions. First, it specifies an artifact protocol that
separates proposal, fresh replay validation, promotion, and post-level refactoring.
Second, it defines an operational marginal-complexity proxy under which unchanged
library routines incur no new cost, positive net file growth is charged, and literal
paths remain visible in the ledger. Third, it reports eight promoted endpoints on a
closed Competition-Mode scorecard, with complete chronological traces for two games and
intermediate artifacts that permit reuse and replay claims to be inspected. These are
case studies of solver-growth accounting; they do not establish a compute-controlled
advantage over other systems.

\section{Related Work and Accounting Framework}
\subsection{Description length and free energy}
The (prefix) Kolmogorov complexity $K(x)$ is the length of the shortest program that
outputs $x$; Solomonoff induction weights hypotheses by $2^{-K}$, formalising Occam's
razor~\cite{kolmogorov1965,solomonoff1964,live2008}. $K$ is uncomputable, but its
description-length principle motivates computable proxies that trade fit against model
size. For the promoted chain $(s_k)$, let $V(s_k)$ denote verified depth and let
$C_{\leq k}$ denote the cumulative positive-growth charge defined in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:marginal-c}. The implemented diagnostic is
\begin{equation}
  \F^{\mathrm{hist}}_\lambda(s_k)
  \;=\; -V(s_k) \;+\; \lambda C_{\leq k},
  \label{eq:fe}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ expresses the reporting scale between behavioral progress and program
growth~\cite{losscomplexity}. Advancement is decided by replay-validated level
completion, not by optimizing Eq.~\eqref{eq:fe}. This historical functional must be
distinguished from the retained-description dual in Eq.~\eqref{eq:model-dual}; we do not
interpret either reported chain as an MDL optimum.

\subsection{Self-modifying programs and interactive ARC agents}
Schmidhuber's G\"odel machine~\cite{schmidhuber2007godel} is an agent that may rewrite
\emph{any} part of its own code, including its learning algorithm, once it finds a proof
that the rewrite increases expected reward. PowerPlay~\cite{schmidhuber2013powerplay}
incrementally searches for the simplest task the system cannot yet solve together with a
solver for it, never forgetting old skills. Artificial curiosity rewards
\emph{compression progress}: transitions that make the agent's world model more
predictable~\cite{schmidhuber2010curiosity}. Our approach adopts the operational parts of this
lineage: the agent revises executable problem-solving code, behavioral improvement is
checked empirically, and retained structure remains inspectable.

ARC-AGI-3 introduces an interactive counterpart to static ARC tasks. Graph-based
exploration represents observed states and transitions explicitly
\cite{arcagi3graph}, while executable-world-model approaches synthesize transition
programs and plan through them~\cite{arcagi3worldmodels,courtis2026opine}. Instead, we
account for the growth of the solver program, regardless of whether a retained routine
is a search procedure, a perceptual transformation, a plan, or a predictive model.

\subsection{Structure functions for solver artifacts}
For finite data $x$, Kolmogorov's classical finite-set structure function is
\begin{equation}
  h_x(\alpha)=\min\{\log |S|:x\in S,\ K(S)\leq\alpha\},
  \label{eq:classical-structure-function}
\end{equation}
which records the smallest residual data-to-model code attainable at model-description
budget $\alpha$~\cite{vereshchagin2004}. Interactive solvers require a behavioral fit
coordinate, and executable description replaces the uncomputable $K(S)$. We therefore
use the inverse, complexity-at-fit parameterization. Let
$V(s)$ be the number of levels reproduced by fresh replay from solver artifact $s$, and
let $D(s)$ be its retained executable description length. For a finite set $\mathcal{P}$
of auditable candidate artifacts, define
\begin{equation}
 \widehat C_{\mathcal{P}}(v)
 =\min\{D(s):s\in\mathcal{P},\;V(s)\geq v\}.
 \label{eq:structure-function}
\end{equation}
This frontier is empirical in two senses: it is restricted to selected programs, and
membership in the constraint set is decided by execution rather than formal proof.
It separates two questions that a level score conflates: whether a
behavioral threshold has been reached, and how much retained executable structure was
required to reach it. The present experiments record verified points in this finite
loss--complexity plane; they do not evaluate Eq.~\eqref{eq:structure-function} over all
preserved proposals, still less compute its uncomputable Kolmogorov ideal.

The marginal trace is the positive variation along the retained construction history,
with an important accounting convention: additions are charged when first admitted,
whereas unchanged shared code is not charged again. Large positive increments indicate
that the retained artifact grew under the chosen implementation; they do not prove that
no shorter incumbent-library composition existed. Small increments indicate little
positive net growth, and support composition only when the source shows calls into
previously charged routines. This turns program history into an observable object rather
than inferring transfer from final accuracy alone.

\subsection{Kolmogorov structure functions and free-energy duality}
The mathematical object is Kolmogorov's structure function. The recent work \cite{losscomplexity} develops its free-energy and Legendre--Fenchel treatment, taking
the whole structure function rather than a single regularized optimum as the primary
object. Let $m$ be the number of levels in a game and define
replay risk by $R(s)=m-V(s)$. The empirical structure function is
\begin{equation}
  C^{*}_{\mathcal P}(r)
  =\inf\{D(s):s\in\mathcal P,\ R(s)\leq r\}.
  \label{eq:model-function}
\end{equation}
It is the least retained description among proposed artifacts achieving risk at most
$r$. Equation~\eqref{eq:structure-function} is the same function in the level-depth
coordinate:
\begin{equation}
  \widehat C_{\mathcal P}(v)=C^{*}_{\mathcal P}(m-v).
  \label{eq:depth-risk-coordinate}
\end{equation}
This equivalence matters experimentally. A final score supplies one horizontal
coordinate; a promoted source snapshot supplies the other. The full clean history
samples a curve in the $(R,D)$ plane.

The free-energy envelope is the one-parameter dual
\begin{align}
  \Phi_{\mathcal P}(\lambda)
    &=\inf_{s\in\mathcal P}\bigl(R(s)+\lambda D(s)\bigr) \\
    &=\inf_r\bigl(r+\lambda C^{*}_{\mathcal P}(r)\bigr),
  \qquad \lambda>0.
  \label{eq:model-dual}
\end{align}
Thus a sweep in $\lambda$ selects supporting lines of slope $-\lambda$ in $(D,R)$
coordinates. Conversely,
\begin{equation}
  \underline C_{\mathcal P}(r)
    =\sup_{\lambda>0}\frac{\Phi_{\mathcal P}(\lambda)-r}{\lambda}
    \leq C^{*}_{\mathcal P}(r)
  \label{eq:model-biconjugate}
\end{equation}
recovers the lower convex envelope visible to linear scalarization. Equality holds on
supported points of the empirical frontier. Where a unique minimizer $s_\lambda$ is
stable, the envelope theorem gives
\begin{equation}
  \Phi'_{\mathcal P}(\lambda)=D(s_\lambda),
  \qquad
  R(s_\lambda)=\Phi_{\mathcal P}(\lambda)
                    -\lambda\Phi'_{\mathcal P}(\lambda).
  \label{eq:model-envelope}
\end{equation}
Kinks therefore mark changes in the selected structural regime. This is the precise
sense in which free-energy scalarization encodes the supported part of the
loss--complexity frontier. Unsupported points require the whole structure function and
cannot be recovered from a linear $\lambda$ sweep alone.

For two candidates $s$ and $s'$, free-energy selection admits the more complex candidate
exactly when its replay improvement pays for its description increase:
\begin{equation}
  R(s)-R(s')>\lambda\bigl(D(s')-D(s)\bigr).
  \label{eq:complexity-ratchet}
\end{equation}
The loss--complexity formulation from \cite{losscomplexity} yields this inequality;
Eq.~\eqref{eq:complexity-ratchet} specializes it to solver artifacts. Our
present promotion rule first requires strict replay progress, so the reported trace is
not a complete $\lambda$ sweep. It is a verified chain that samples the structure function.
On a nested chain with nondecreasing retained descriptions and one clean artifact per
depth, the frontier increment
$\widehat C_{\mathcal P}(k)-\widehat C_{\mathcal P}(k-1)$ equals the retained-description
increment. With refactors, the pathwise positive variation in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:marginal-c} retains the historical price even when $D$ later falls. The
two quantities answer different questions: the structure function asks for the shortest
retained representative, while the ledger asks when new structure was paid for.

\subsection{Categorical formulation}
Let $E$ expose observations in $O$, actions in $A$, and finite interaction histories
$H_E\subseteq(O\times A)^*\times O$. Let $Q$ be the set of solver-owned control states
(including a program counter, search frontier, or dispatcher state), and put
$Z=H_E\times Q$. This construction records only observable history and solver-owned
memory; it does not expose hidden environment state. A partial policy is a map
$p:U_p\to A$ on some $U_p\subseteq Z$. Search procedures, reactive routines, and
literal paths induce such policies after their finite control state is made explicit,
although their program descriptions can have very different lengths.

\begin{definition}
The category $\mathsf{Pol}(E)$ has partial policies as objects and a unique morphism
$p\to q$ when $U_p\subseteq U_q$ and $q|_{U_p}=p$. Thus morphisms are
trace-preserving policy extensions. A family $(p_i)_{i\in I}$ is compatible when
$p_i(x)=p_j(x)$ for every $x\in U_{p_i}\cap U_{p_j}$.
\end{definition}
This is a partially ordered set regarded as a category; colimits therefore coincide
with least upper bounds when they exist~\cite{maclane1998}.

\begin{theorem}[Compatible-policy colimit]
Every compatible family $(p_i)_{i\in I}$ in $\mathsf{Pol}(E)$ has a colimit. Its
domain is $U=\bigcup_i U_{p_i}$ and its action map is the union
$p=\bigcup_i p_i$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Compatibility makes $p$ single-valued on overlaps. Each inclusion $p_i\to p$ is a
morphism. If $q$ is any other cocone, then $q$ extends every $p_i$, hence extends their
union $p$ and determines a morphism $p\to q$. Uniqueness follows because
$\mathsf{Pol}(E)$ is a poset category.
\end{proof}

The theorem is elementary and carries no empirical conclusion by itself. Its relevance
begins at the representation level. A shared leg
describes a partial policy intensionally; a level player supplies dispatch, sequencing,
and boundary conditions that make selected policies compatible on the states actually
encountered. New glue resolves overlaps or fills states not covered by the existing
diagram. A debrief that replaces repeated literal actions by a parameterized ferry or
search routine changes the program description of the same observed partial policy
union. The behavioral colimit can therefore remain fixed while its structure-function
value falls.

Arbitrary Python programs do not expose their full policy domains, and replay visits
only a finite trace. Consequently, our method constructs a finite empirical diagram: the
objects are observed leg behaviors, the cocone is the executable player, and commuting
conditions are tested on replayed transitions. The construction is an under-approximation
to $\mathsf{Pol}(E)$, but it is not metaphorical: the source names the component maps,
and the action trace supplies the tested states. Failed replay falsifies a proposed
behavioral extension, although trace inspection is needed to localize whether dispatch,
coverage, or overlap compatibility failed. Equation~\eqref{eq:marginal-c} records the
positive net source growth of the chosen empirical extension from level $k-1$ to level
$k$; it does not compute the minimum description required for such an extension.

The categorical construction also suggests a natural ideal two-part description code.
Let
$L_k$ be the set of shared legs available through depth $k$, and let $g_i$ encode the
level-$i$ player: selected leg names, argument bindings, order, and residual glue. Up to
a fixed interpreter and self-delimiting headers, the joint cone code is
\begin{equation}
  D_{\mathrm{cone}}(k)
   =\sum_{\ell\in L_k}D(\ell)
    +\sum_{i=1}^{k}D(g_i\mid L_i)+O(\log k).
  \label{eq:cone-code}
\end{equation}
If existing leg definitions remain unchanged, extending the cone by one level costs
\begin{equation}
  \Delta D_{\mathrm{cone}}(k)
   =\sum_{\ell\in L_k\setminus L_{k-1}}D(\ell)
    +D(g_k\mid L_k)+O(\log k).
  \label{eq:cone-marginal}
\end{equation}
This equation states the ideal reuse claim precisely: an old leg is paid for once, while
each new use pays only its binding and composition code. In the implemented surrogate,
a container literal remains charged by its elements rather than becoming free merely
because it occupies one source line. If a debrief
replaces several literal gluings by one parameterized leg, the compression gain at
fixed verified behavior is
\begin{equation}
  G_k=D_{\mathrm{cone}}^{\mathrm{before}}(k)
      -D_{\mathrm{cone}}^{\mathrm{after}}(k).
  \label{eq:compression-gain}
\end{equation}
The empirical structure function registers $G_k>0$ as a shorter representative; the
historical marginal ledger does not erase the earlier construction cost. This is the
mathematical distinction between compression of an artifact and retroactive deletion
of provenance. Equations~\eqref{eq:cone-code}--\eqref{eq:compression-gain} specify a
description schema; Eq.~\eqref{eq:marginal-c} is only a coarse source-level surrogate
for changes in that schema.

\section{Method}
\subsection{Questions and unit of analysis}
The unit of analysis is a \emph{promoted solver transition}: a change from one clean,
replay-validated artifact to another that clears at least one additional level. We ask
three artifact-level questions. Does a later level compose previously admitted routines
or introduce new executable structure? Can literal replay, new mechanics, and later
abstraction be distinguished in the source history? Does the same protocol remain
meaningful across games with different mechanics and marginal profiles? These questions
concern attribution and auditability rather than global optimality. The observational
object is the sequence of verified artifacts and the maps between them.

\subsection{Interface and proposer}
The game-independent interface exposes only
\begin{center}
\texttt{step(action)}$\to$\emph{frame}, \; \emph{frame} (raw $64\times64$ grid),
\; \emph{levels\_completed} (reward), \\ \; and \texttt{clone()} (lookahead).
\end{center}
No interface field identifies objects, mechanics, or goals. The coding proposer is
nevertheless not prior-free. Its prompt states general expectations about persistent
objects, barriers, controllable and autonomous entities, sparse reward, and experimental
probing. It receives file and shell tools and may interact with cloned environments while
writing a \texttt{solve(env)} program. The reported artifacts were produced by a strong
coding-agent proposer; an earlier prompt-only local model did not produce a successful
trace. Proposer model calls and exploratory interactions were not normalized across
levels and are not included in the complexity measure.

The proposer works in a disposable directory seeded from the latest clean promotion.
It may write probes, inspect frames, execute candidates, and revise source. Temporary
work can be much larger than the promoted program. The retained-complexity measure
concerns the executable structure carried forward to subsequent levels; generation cost
and interaction cost remain separate resources and are not silently folded into $C_k$.

A later campaign exposed why proposer compliance cannot be assumed from instructions.
After observational probing stalled on \texttt{ft09}, a Sonnet API proposer emitted two
separate commands accessing \texttt{env.\_game} and enumerating the private runtime.
We classify these
as operational cheating attempts because they seek evidence outside the declared
frame/action interface. The attempts were retained only as dirty WIP and are excluded
from every promotion and result in this paper. The first exact transcript is preserved
under \texttt{agent\_solutions/ft09\_legs/wip\_context/level\_01/}
\texttt{interrupted\_a9a30e6e4da1}. Their recurrence suggests that instruction
compliance can deteriorate when observational progress stalls. The harness therefore
blocks recognized private-runtime and hidden-source access before execution and
independently rejects tainted workspaces before replay or promotion; prompt wording
alone is not an audit boundary.

\subsection{Program structure and promotion protocol}
The workspace separates reusable and level-specific code. The module
\texttt{legs.py} contains shared routines, \texttt{players.py} contains one thin entry
point per level, and \texttt{solve.py} dispatches according to observed level progress.
For level $k$, the harness first attempts to compose existing routines. If this fails,
the proposer may add routines and level-specific glue. A candidate that reaches level
$k$ is run again, and its recorded action sequence is replayed from a fresh environment.
Only a replay-validated state is promoted. A separate debrief may then move repeated
structure into the shared library, after which the solver is validated again. Failed,
interrupted, pre-debrief, and post-debrief workspaces are preserved as provenance rather
than merged into the promoted solver.

The promotion protocol has six stages: seed a workspace from the highest clean
artifact; attempt composition from existing legs; permit a proposer to add source when
reuse fails; execute and independently replay the candidate; measure and promote the
validated delta; then debrief repeated glue into a higher-order leg and replay again.
Known failed reuse attempts are keyed by level and library hash, so a restart does not
repeat an identical expensive search. A proposer-mutated checkpoint is treated as an
untrusted path candidate until the harness validates it. This order prevents edits that
never solved a level, stale checkpoints, and unrefactored literal paths from being
misreported as admitted abstractions.

\subsection{Marginal-complexity measure}
Let $d(f)$ be the number of nonblank, noncomment source lines in file $f$, plus the
number of elements in Python list, tuple, set, and dictionary literals found by parsing
its abstract syntax tree. The literal term prevents a long action sequence on one source
line from receiving unit cost. If $\ell_k$ and $p_k$ are the shared-library and player
sources after level $k$, the recorded marginal cost is
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
 &C_k = [d(\ell_k)-d(\ell_{k-1})]_+ + [d(p_k)-d(p_{k-1})]_+, \\
 &\text{where } [x]_+ ={} \max(0,x).
\end{split}
 \label{eq:marginal-c}
\end{equation}
The cumulative ledger is $C_{\leq k}=\sum_{i\leq k}C_i$. Unchanged shared code
therefore has zero marginal cost. New routines, glue, and container literals increase
the statistic only to the extent that their file's total description size grows. This
is a coarse, language-dependent proxy rather than Kolmogorov complexity:
it ignores identifier length, comments, \texttt{solve.py}, temporary probes, model
inference, environment interactions, and deleted code. Additions can cancel deletions
within a file before the positive part is applied, and no negative credit is assigned
when a refactor reduces source size. We use $\lambda=0.02$ only when displaying
the diagnostic value in Eq.~\eqref{eq:fe}.

Writing $D_k=d(\ell_k)+d(p_k)$, the two quantities have a precise relation. If neither
file shrinks, then $C_{\leq k}=D_k-D_0$; with deletions or compressive refactors,
$C_{\leq k}\geq D_k-D_0$. Thus $D_k$ locates the retained artifact in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:structure-function}, whereas $C_{\leq k}$ records the pathwise
construction charge. The experiments emphasize the latter because the question is when
structure entered the verified history.

The implementation follows this definition directly. It counts nonblank, noncomment
lines, parses Python with the standard abstract-syntax-tree parser, and adds one unit for
each container-literal element. The positive-part operator makes accumulated recorded
charges monotone, but it does not guarantee that every introduced structure was charged.
A low $C_k$ supports reuse only when the
player invokes previously charged behavior and the composed solver passes replay;
therefore source provenance and behavioral validation are reported together.

\subsection{Experimental and artifact scope}
The study reports eight canonical promoted artifacts. Two games, \texttt{wa30} and
\texttt{ls20}, have complete manuscript-level construction histories; five further
games have replay-valid artifacts through level~4, and \texttt{tu93} has one promoted
level. The study does not report independent seeds or confidence intervals. The primary
endpoints are the highest level reproduced by fresh replay, replay action count, and the
recorded sequence $(C_k)$. Promoted code, checkpoints, action paths, and intermediate
workspaces are available in the repository
\footnote{\href{https://github.com/sashakolpakov/gkm}{Project repository and artifacts available at https://github.com/sashakolpakov/gkm}.}.
The replay procedure is documented in
\texttt{REPRODUCE\_ARC.md}. This procedure reproduces the promoted behavior and ledger;
it does not reproduce the stochastic and externally hosted coding-agent calls that
generated the history.

\subsection{Artifact manifest and falsifiability}
Each promoted game directory contains the shared library, per-level players, dispatcher,
checkpoint, run summary, and debrief log. The checkpoint stores verified depth,
cumulative marginal cost, transition records, the final action path, and validation
status. The \texttt{wip\_context} tree retains reached-before-debrief, recovered-path,
and after-debrief states. Git history retains earlier clean promotions, including the
complete early \texttt{wa30} records used below.

Every central empirical claim has a concrete countertest. Fresh replay can falsify depth
or action count. Where adjacent clean snapshots exist, recomputing
Eq.~\eqref{eq:marginal-c} can falsify a ledger entry. The first three \texttt{wa30}
levels were committed together; their contemporaneous rows and clean-through-L3 source
are preserved as one promotion event, and the manifest exposes that granularity rather
than inventing intermediate Git commits. Inspecting the player call graph can falsify
reuse attribution, and comparing pre- and post-debrief source can falsify a claimed
refactor. The manuscript is therefore an index to executable evidence; the repository
is the primary result object.

\subsection{The \texttt{wa30} artifact}
The promoted \texttt{wa30} checkpoint records nine levels reached, successful fresh
replay, and a 596-action path. Its solver is not solely an action trace. Early player
entries call movement, interaction, and relay routines; levels~5--8 compose transport,
neutralization, courier-yield, and target-filling routines; and level~9 is represented
as five calls through a shared \texttt{ferry\_each} interface. The preserved level-9
history is particularly informative: a verified 61-action suffix first appears as a
literal plan and is subsequently represented as repeated grab--carry--release
operations through \texttt{grab\_carry\_release} and \texttt{ferry\_each}.

The canonical manuscript history supplies a value for every level:
\begin{equation}
  (C_1,\ldots,C_9)=(112,78,95,47,405,225,145,204,147).
  \label{eq:wa30-ledger}
\end{equation}
The first four records are retained in the clean
July 2 promotions, and the remaining records are retained in the successive deep
promotions through level~9. Git commits \texttt{15c0a049}, \texttt{0ccea257},
\texttt{875e5b86}, and \texttt{07a1885} make the progression directly inspectable.
The current root checkpoint is deliberately an operational resume artifact and contains
only its post-base records (1243 units); the sidecar joins those records to the earlier
clean Git evidence to form the complete 1458-unit publication ledger.
The large level-5 increment is followed by lower but still substantial costs because
later levels continue to add logistics structure rather than becoming simple layout
variants. Levels~6--8 nevertheless share a higher-order ``neutralize, then deliver''
shape, while the level-9 debrief compresses a recovered suffix into a ferry batch.

The historical record also contains a neutral-prior level-1 replication with cost 181
and a Sonnet-produced replication with cost 164. These replications are evidence that
the carry mechanic can be rediscovered under different proposer conditions; they are
not substituted into the main chronological trace. The reported value 112 belongs to
the promoted progression used for the complete ledger.

\subsection{The \texttt{ls20} artifact}
The promoted \texttt{ls20} checkpoint records seven levels reached, successful fresh
replay, a 393-action path, and a complete seven-entry marginal ledger totaling 362
units. Its early levels are sliding-track puzzles rather than the transport tasks in
\texttt{wa30}. The first solver uses full-frame search. When display noise makes raw
frames unsuitable as search keys, the level-3 solver introduces a masking routine;
level~4 reuses that routine with three units of player glue. Levels~5--7 retain verified
plans recovered during proposal, including routines associated in the debriefs with a
combination display, energy management, moving hazards, and partial observability.

\subsection{The marginal ledger accounting}

\subsection{Interpretation of the complete histories}
The two games give complementary profiles. In \texttt{ls20}, levels~2 and~4 are close
to the ideal colimit-extension case: a previously admitted search behavior covers the
new states and the player supplies only a small amount of glue. Level~3 changes the
effective state representation because a drifting display invalidates raw-frame
identity; the cost increase is therefore attached to a new quotient of observation
space, not merely a longer route. Levels~5--7 contain explicit plan information, so the
ledger correctly keeps their cost visible even though replay succeeds.

The \texttt{wa30} sequence tests a different regime. Level~1 establishes movement,
interaction, transport, and perception. Level~2 adds an autonomous helper; level~3
adds asymmetric relay across a barrier; and level~4 reuses the helper--relay mechanism,
producing the early drop from 95 to 47. Level~5 requires live-frame courier pacing and
is the largest increment in the complete trace. Levels~6--8 vary the agent that must be
neutralized and the delivery topology, but the debrief identifies a shared two-stage
control shape. Level~9 first enters as a verified suffix and is then factored into a
parameterized ferry batch. Thus a downward marginal trend is not expected in general:
the profile falls when an empirical policy colimit extends cheaply and rises when the
domain, compatibility conditions, or retained plan information changes.

\subsection{Bounded cross-game campaign}
To test whether these complete solves depended on per-game architecture, we applied the
same game-agnostic harness and promotion protocol in short bounded runs and stopped five
further games at level~4: \texttt{ft09}, \texttt{g50t}, \texttt{r11l}, \texttt{sp80},
and \texttt{tr87}. Figure~\ref{fig:bounded-profiles} shows their level-1--4 marginal
profiles on a common scale. These additional sawtooth profiles place low net-growth
transitions beside larger retained changes rather than enforcing monotone decline. A
repository-wide taint
scan found every promoted root clean, and fresh local replay plus an
\href{https://arcprize.org/scorecards/9e166671-0953-42f3-89de-a0fd57d7b147}{official
Competition-Mode replay} validated all five level-4 endpoints as well as the complete
\texttt{wa30} and \texttt{ls20} paths and the promoted \texttt{tu93} level. ``Unchanged
architecture'' here means the same interface, proposer loop, taint boundary, replay
gate, and promotion rule; the learned solver files are game-specific artifacts. Since
this protocol solved two games completely and reached level~4 on five more before the
imposed stops, progress beyond level~4 appears primarily to require additional proposer
compute rather than a new hand-designed per-game architecture. This is an operational
extrapolation from bounded public games, not a proof of benchmark-wide scaling.

This interpretation yields two testable predictions for future games. Repeated layout
variants under stable mechanics should produce low marginal increments after the first
successful abstraction. A mechanic change should produce an increment whose source
diff localizes to new state representation, control, or glue. A scalar score makes
neither prediction inspectable; the promoted transition history does.

\section{Artifact-Level Comparison}
\subsection{Contemporary ARC-AGI-3 systems}
The ARC-AGI-3 report lists
public-environment harnesses solving \texttt{ls20}, \texttt{ft09}, and \texttt{vc33};
its paper source does not mention \texttt{wa30}~\cite{arcagi3}. The graph-exploration
baseline evaluates \texttt{ls20} but not \texttt{wa30}; at its 4{,}000-interaction
comparison point it solves 2 levels on \texttt{ls20} and reports that levels~3+ become
intractable for exhaustive exploration~\cite{arcagi3graph}. The closest comparator is
the executable-world-model baseline, which reports \texttt{ls20} 7/7 with both of its
models and \texttt{wa30} 7/9 versus 4/9 depending on the
model~\cite{arcagi3worldmodels}. These figures establish neither an advantage nor a
disadvantage for our approach because proposer models, interaction budgets, and evaluation
conditions differ. The useful point of comparison is artifact scope: our harness adds a
source-growth ledger and intermediate refactoring history to its promoted replays.

\subsection{A common artifact audit}
Executable agents can be compared without pretending that unlike complexity units are
equal. Four questions apply to either solver programs or synthesized world models:
(i) what executable object is retained after a level; (ii) which behavioral claim is
independently replayed; (iii) how is new source or data attributed to the active level;
and (iv) what evidence shows that later behavior factors through earlier structure?

\subsection{OPINE-World artifact audit}
OPINE-World calls its synthesized \texttt{game\_engine.py} an executable world
model~\cite{courtis2026opine}. For the present audit, that term requires more than an
executable replay surrogate. A world-model artifact must expose a compact transition
account that generates level behavior from shared rules; it must not depend on loading
the observed entry frame for each level as an external fact. Its revision history should
also reveal compression or charged reuse when later levels factor through earlier
structure. The published \texttt{wa30} artifact fails both tests.

First, the final transition function explicitly loads
\texttt{l\%d\_initial.pkl} on reset and on a level-completing transition. It therefore
does not generate the nine level-entry states from its learned transition structure.
Each of the nine final caches is an 8,464-byte verbatim state object, for 76,176 bytes
of retained level-entry data. Across the archive, 389 such caches occupy 3,292,496 raw
bytes. This is valid replay support for a previously observed environment, but it is
not a compressed generative account of the game.

Second, all 65 synthesis snapshots are recoverable, so the absence of a compression
profile is measurable rather than conjectural. Writing $A_k$ for diff-added source at
level $k$, the measured sequence is
\begin{equation}
 (A_1,\ldots,A_9)
 =(3243,3978,1712,2053,1246,1136,266,3310,1444),
 \qquad \sum_k A_k=18388.
 \label{eq:opine-added}
\end{equation}
Every level admits hundreds or thousands of new tokens. The 266-token minimum
at level~7 is not a reuse tooth: it remains a substantial source addition and still
ships a separate entry-state cache. Variation in synthesis burden is not the previously
discussed sawtooth, whose troughs are tied to named unchanged legs and near-zero glue.

Nor is there a compression ``tooth'' in retained size. Source grows from 1,882 to 14,161
tokens, or 7.52 times its initial size. Only 8 of 64 consecutive revisions shrink, and
the largest reduction is 222 tokens, merely 1.6\% of the final program. No revision
refactors accumulated level cases into a substantially smaller shared account. The
published object grows by accretion.

\paragraph{Audit verdict.}
The measurable OPINE \texttt{wa30} artifact is a cumulative simulator plus cached
level-entry frames. It can be executed and used for planning, but execution alone does
not make it a world model in the structure-function sense. The archive exhibits neither
a charged reuse sawtooth nor a compression event, and it does not generate its own level
initial conditions. Consequently, the published artifact does not substantiate the
claim that OPINE learned a compressed generative world model; it substantiates an
accumulated replay model of the observed game.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
\subsection{Interpretation}
The artifact protocol answers a narrower question than whether a system has learned a
general world model. It records where executable solver structure resides and whether a
later player invokes previously charged code, adds new source, or retains a literal
plan. In \texttt{ls20}, that distinction is visible in a complete chronological ledger.
In \texttt{wa30}, the late level-9 history demonstrates a second useful distinction:
behavioral replay can validate a literal plan before the debrief identifies a reusable
transport pattern. Treating those two representations as equivalent would conceal the
structural change.

\subsection{Colimits as compression events}
The categorical and algorithmic accounts meet at debrief time. Before refactoring, a
literal suffix describes a policy only on the states of one verified trace. A
parameterized leg describes a larger partial policy and supplies maps from several
level-specific instances into one shared object. When the new description replays the
old traces, the refactor is a compression event: behavioral coverage is preserved while
the factorization becomes reusable. The level-9 \texttt{ferry\_each} change is the
clearest instance in the repository. Conversely, a thin player that merely calls an
unchanged search leg is already expressed in the incumbent colimit and needs little new
description.

The ledger deliberately charges additions rather than estimating semantic novelty from
model activations or prose. This gives it a stable audit surface. A reviewer can locate
the exact source introduced at a transition, decide whether the claimed abstraction is
present, and run the composite. The price is representation dependence: a Python source
measure is not an invariant of behavior. Equation~\eqref{eq:structure-function} makes
the correct response explicit: compare multiple descriptions that attain the same
verified threshold. The present repository supplies the first such points, including
literal and refactored variants; a broader structure-function sweep is a natural next
experiment.

\subsection{Repository as the contribution surface}
For a self-revising coding agent, a final source file omits the central scientific
object: the sequence of proposals, validations, promotions, and compressions. The GitHub repository retains this sequence at three resolutions. Checkpoints provide machine-
readable behavioral and complexity records; Git promotions provide clean chronological
states; WIP snapshots show how recovered paths became admitted programs and later
abstractions. The paper supplies definitions and interpretation, but the evidential
weight lies in those executable transitions.

This changes what replication means. Exact regeneration of stochastic coding-agent
transcripts is useful for cost studies but unnecessary for testing the promoted claims.
An independent implementation can replay the paths, recompute source deltas, and inspect
whether the named legs factor the stated behaviors. It can then substitute a different
proposer or complexity code while preserving the artifact protocol. The method is thus
separable from the particular model that generated the current repository.

\subsection{Scope and validity}
The claims are deliberately artifact-scoped: eight promoted replay endpoints, two
complete per-level histories, and an audit of a published comparator archive. They are
falsifiable by the repository procedures above. The closed scorecard is a public
benchmark result, but it does not establish a success frequency for future games or a
compute-matched cross-system ranking; neither assertion is needed for the measured
replay, provenance, and marginal-complexity results to be valid.

\section{Conclusion}
Aggregate task completion cannot distinguish reusable solver growth from the retention
of task-specific code or action traces. Our approach makes that distinction inspectable by
combining fresh replay, a constrained shared-library structure, preserved intermediate
states, and a marginal source ledger. Eight promoted endpoints, including two complete
games, show that the protocol can record both near-zero-growth routine reuse and costly
literal or mechanic-specific additions. The Kolmogorov structure-function view places
each clean solver in an
empirical behavior--description plane; the categorical view identifies reusable competence
with compatible partial policies and their empirical colimit. Together they turn a
self-improving solver from a final score into an auditable growth object. The primary
contribution is the replayable repository of that growth, with the manuscript providing
its formal and empirical account.
